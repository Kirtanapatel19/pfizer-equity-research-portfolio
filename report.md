### 5.3 Gordon Growth Model / Dividend Discount Model (Required Section)

Pfizer's dividend relevance warrants a DDM cross-check.

Illustrative DDM setup:

1. Next-period dividend \(D_1\): aligned with current annualized trajectory.
2. Required return \(k_e\): CAPM-derived cost of equity with policy/pipeline risk consideration.
3. Long-run dividend growth \(g\): conservative, post-LOE normalized growth.

Formula:

\[
P_0 = \frac{D_1}{k_e - g}
\]

DDM is used as a support method, not as sole valuation basis.

## 8.0 Field Research (Pages 40-43 Style, Q&A Format)

### 8.1 Interview Context

I interviewed Shivani (Early Clinical Development, Oncology, Pfizer Inc.) as part of this research.  
Interview date: April 17, 2026.  
Purpose: Validate assumptions about pipeline prioritization, clinical attrition, combination strategy, and AI-enabled development workflows.

At the interviewee's request, this section reflects high-level, non-confidential observations only.

### 8.2 Interview Q&A

**Q1: How does Pfizer prioritize which drug candidates move forward through trial phases?**  
**Shivani:** The process is high-risk/high-reward and starts with a landscape scan. Teams evaluate what is currently approved, what physicians are using off-label, and what is emerging in clinical data from conferences like ASCO and ESMO. Program selection then depends on internal molecule fit: whether a molecule can work across tumor types, across lines of therapy, and in combination settings. Internal competition is also a major factor because overlapping molecules may target similar patient populations.

**Q2: Why do some promising programs still get deprioritized or terminated?**  
**Shivani:** Deprioritization is often a portfolio decision rather than proof a molecule has no value. If one internal program has stronger data in a target indication, resources are reallocated. In early stage, attrition is expected: of multiple programs she has worked on, only one advanced to pivotal stage while others were terminated.

**Q3: How should investors interpret this attrition dynamic?**  
**Shivani:** Terminations are part of normal early-stage R&D economics. They should be interpreted as disciplined capital allocation unless failures cluster around one platform or mechanism.

**Q4: On bispecific approaches (e.g., PD-1/VEGF families), what is the strategic rationale?**  
**Shivani:** The rationale is synergy: targeting two validated pathways in one construct could produce stronger anti-tumor activity than either mechanism alone. The field is very competitive and timing to positive data is crucial.

**Q5: Where do you see the biggest breakthrough potential in targeted therapies (ADCs/bispecifics)?**  
**Shivani:** In ADCs, linker chemistry remains a major innovation lever (including tripeptide/dipeptide linker design), with the objective of improving efficacy while reducing off-target toxicity. Another breakthrough area is combination strategy being designed early rather than tested only after monotherapy programs mature.

**Q6: How is AI changing clinical-development workflows?**  
**Shivani:** AI is already helpful in exploratory ways: protocol drafting support, informed-consent drafting support, recruitment planning, and target triage. It improves speed and efficiency, but it is not replacing scientific decision-making today. Most current use is still exploratory and complementary.

**Q7: Is Pfizer actively using combination-first strategy in study design?**  
**Shivani:** Yes. Rather than defaulting to a slower monotherapy-first pathway, teams increasingly evaluate combination potential early (including with approved agents and internal assets) to improve probability of meaningful clinical outcomes.

**Q8: What should investors monitor as evidence of strong execution?**  
**Shivani:** Investors should monitor milestone discipline, quality of data at key readouts, and consistency of program progression decisions. These indicators matter more than one-off headlines.

### 8.3 Analyst Interpretation and Model Link

1. Internal portfolio competition supports conservative conversion assumptions for early pipeline assets.  
2. Combination-first development supports a higher confidence level for selected late-stage assets versus legacy monotherapy assumptions.  
3. Program attrition is modeled as expected base-rate behavior, not an automatic thesis break.  
4. AI/next-gen modality comments are treated as long-term optionality, not near-term base-case drivers.
